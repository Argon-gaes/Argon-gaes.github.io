7.2 s Orbitals Are Spherically Symmetric

The hydrogen atomic wave functions depend upon three quantum numbers, nnn, lll, and mlm_lml​. The quantum number nnn is called the principal quantum number and has the values 1,2,⋯1,2,\cdots1,2,⋯ The energy of the hydrogen atom depends upon only the principal quantum number through the equation En=−e2/8πϵ0a0n2E_n=-e^2/8\pi\epsilon_0a_0n^2En​=−e2/8πϵ0​a0​n2.

The quantum number lll is called the angular-momentum quantum number and has the values 0,1,⋯ ,n−10,1,\cdots,n-10,1,⋯,n−1. The magnitude of the angular momentum of the electron about the proton is determined completely by lll through ∣L∣=ℏl(l+1)|L|=\hbar\sqrt {l(l+1)}∣L∣=ℏl(l+1)​.

Note that the form of the radial wave functions depends upon both nnn and lll. The value of I is customarily denoted by a letter, with l=0l=0l=0 being denoted by sss, l=1l=1l=1 by ppp, l=2l=2l=2 by ddd, l=3l=3l=3 by fff. The origin of the letters s,p,d,fs,p,d,fs,p,d,f is historic and has to do with the designation of the observed spectral lines of atomic sodium. (The letters s,p,ds,p,ds,p,d and fff stand for sharp, principal, diffuse, and fundamental.)

The third quantum number mlm_lml​ is called the magnetic quantum number and takes on the 2l+12l+12l+1 values ml=0,±1,±2,⋯m_l=0,\pm1,\pm2,\cdotsml​=0,±1,±2,⋯. The z component of the angular momentum is determined completely by mlm_lml​ through Lz=mlℏL_z=m_l\hbarLz​=ml​ℏ. The quantum number mlm_lml​ is called the magnetic quantum number because the energy of a hydrogen atom in a magnetic field depends on mlm_lml​. In the absence of a magnetic field, each energy level has a degeneracy of 2l+12l+12l+1. In the presence of a magnetic field, these levels split, and the energy depends upon the particular value of mlm_lml​. This splitting is illustrated in Figure 7.2 and is called the Zeeman effect, which is discussed in Section 7.4.

7.4 The Energy Levels of a Hydrogen Atom Are Split by a Magnetic Field

In this section, we shall discuss a hydrogen atom in an external magnetic field. We shall see that the magnetic field causes the energy levels to be split into sublevels, which leads to a splitting of the spectral lines in hydrogen. This splitting is called the Zeeman effect.

We shall review some facts and equations concerning magnetic dipoles and magnetic fields:

m=q2mL\boldsymbol m=\frac {q}{2m}\boldsymbol L m=2mq​L

For an electron, q=−∣e∣q=-|e|q=−∣e∣:

m=−∣e∣2meL\boldsymbol m=-\frac {|e|}{2m_e}\boldsymbol L m=−2me​∣e∣​L

A magnetic dipole will interact with a magnetic field, and the potential energy of a magnetic dipole in a magnetic field is given by:

V=−m⋅BV=-\boldsymbol m \cdot \boldsymbol B V=−m⋅B

We take the magnetic field to be in the z direction:

V=−mzBzV=-m_zB_z V=−mz​Bz​

With the equation of mzm_zmz​:

V=∣e∣Bz2meLzV=\frac {|e|B_z}{2m_e}L_z V=2me​∣e∣Bz​​Lz​

If we replace LzL_zLz​ by its operator equivalent L^z\hat L_zL^z​, then the equation gives the part of the hydrogen atom Hamiltonian operator that accounts for the external magnetic field. Thus, the Hamiltonian operator for a hydrogen atom in an external magnetic field is:

A state with given values of nnn and lll is split into 2l+12l+12l+1 levels by an external magnetic field.

（ 当存在外磁场时，系统总能量需要再加上在磁场中的势能，所以能级会进一步分裂，能级分裂项数与mlm_lml​的取值有关，共2l+12l+12l+1项。

不过，上述分析仍存在缺陷，还没有考虑电子自旋产生的影响，实际的光谱线更为复杂，见Section 7.5。 ）

7.5 An Electron Has an Intrinsic Spin Angular Momentum

In 1922, two German physicists, Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach, passed a beam of silver atoms (recall from general chemistry that a silver atom has a 4d105s14d^{10}5s^14d105s1 outer electron configuration, and so has a single outer electron) through an inhomogeneous magnetic field in order to split the beam into its 2l+12l+12l+1 space-quantized components.

Stern and Gerlach found the quite unexpected result that a beam of silver atoms splits into only two parts. Note that this corresponds to 2l+1=22l+1=22l+1=2, or to l=1/2l=1/2l=1/2. Up to now we have admitted only integer values of lll.

Another similar observation is the splitting that occurs in atomic spectra. For example, under high resolution it was observed that the n=2n=2n=2 to n=1n=1n=1 transition in atomic hydrogen is split into two closely spaced lines, called a doublet. These observations cannot be explained using the ideas and equations that we have developed up to now.

In 1925, Wolfgang Pauli showed that all these observations could be explained with the postulate that an electron can exist in two distinct states. Pauli introduced a fourth quantum number in a rather ad hoc manner.

This fourth quantum number, now called the spin quantum number msm_sms​, is restricted to the two values +12+\frac 12+21​ and −12-\frac 12−21​. It is interesting that Pauli did not give any interpretation to this fourth quantum number. The existence of a fourth quantum number was somewhat of a mystery because the three spatial coordinates of an electron account for nnn, lll, and mlm_lml​, but what is this quantum number due to?

In the early 1930s, the British physicist Paul Dirac developed a relativistic extension of quantum mechanics, and one of its greaitest successes is that spin arose in a perfectly natural way.

We shall introduce spin here, however, in an ad hoc manner.

（ 前面是科学史环节，简单说就是现有的理论无法解释谱线分裂的实验现象，因此波尔提出了自旋磁量子数的概念，但是仍然没有给出合理的理论解释。直到狄拉克提出了狄拉克方程，才从相对论量子力学的角度解释了自旋现象。当然，初等量子力学一般不会讲狄拉克方程。 ）

We define the spin operator S^2\hat S^2S^2 and S^z\hat S_zS^z​ and their eigenfunctions α\alphaα and β\betaβ by the equations:

S^2α=ℏ2s(s+1)αS^2β=ℏ2s(s+1)βs=12S^zα=12ℏαS^zβ=−12ℏβ\hat S^2\alpha=\hbar^2s(s+1)\alpha \quad \hat S^2\beta=\hbar^2s(s+1)\beta \quad s=\frac 12 \\[3mm] \hat S_z\alpha=\frac 12\hbar\alpha \quad \hat S_z\beta=-\frac 12\hbar\beta S^2α=ℏ2s(s+1)αS^2β=ℏ2s(s+1)βs=21​S^z​α=21​ℏαS^z​β=−21​ℏβ

We can say that the spin angular momentum of an electron is:

S=ℏs(s+1)S=\hbar\sqrt{s(s+1)} S=ℏs(s+1)​

Unlike lll, which can vary from 0 to ∞\infin∞, sss can have only the value s=12s=\frac 12s=21​. Note that because sss is not allowed to assume large values, the spin angular momentum can never assume classical behavior. Spin is strictly a nonclassical concept. The functions α\alphaα and β\betaβ are called spin eigenfunctions. We assume that α\alphaα and β\betaβ are orthonormal, which we write formally as:

⟨α∣α⟩=⟨β∣β⟩=1⟨α∣β⟩=⟨β∣α⟩=0\langle \alpha|\alpha \rangle=\langle \beta|\beta \rangle=1 \\ \langle \alpha|\beta \rangle=\langle \beta|\alpha \rangle=0 ⟨α∣α⟩=⟨β∣β⟩=1⟨α∣β⟩=⟨β∣α⟩=0

Ψn,l,ml,ms(r,θ,ϕ,σ)=ψn,l,ml(r,θ,ϕ)α(σ)or∣n l ml ms⟩\Psi_{n,l,m_l,m_s}(r,\theta,\phi,\sigma)=\psi_{n,l,m_l}(r,\theta,\phi)\alpha(\sigma) \quad or \quad|n\ l\ m_l\ m_s \rangle Ψn,l,ml​,ms​​(r,θ,ϕ,σ)=ψn,l,ml​​(r,θ,ϕ)α(σ)or∣n l ml​ ms​⟩

Thus, four quantum numbers are required to specify the state of an electron in a hydrogen atom. The complete one-electron wave function ∣n l ml ms⟩|n\ l\ m_l\ m_s \rangle∣n l ml​ ms​⟩ is called a spin orbital.

（ 自旋是完全量子化的概念，它无法对应经典的性质。尽管不那么直观，我们还是可以用很多物理或数学的方式来描述它。至于自旋到底是什么，只能说它是粒子的一种内禀性质。 ）

7.6 Spin-Orbit Interaction Affects the Energies of a Hydrogen Atom

We saw in Section 7.4 that an electron has a magnetic moment that is proportional to its orbital angular momentum, or that:

mL=−∣e∣2meL\boldsymbol m_L =-\frac {|e|}{2m_e}\boldsymbol L mL​=−2me​∣e∣​L

There is also a magnetic moment associated with the spin of the electron. In order to obtain agreement with a number of experimental observations, we must modify the equation by introducing a factor ggg and write:

mS=−g∣e∣2meS\boldsymbol m_S=-\frac {g|e|}{2m_e}\boldsymbol S mS​=−2me​g∣e∣​S

This Hamiltonian operator yields the energies En=−mee4/8ϵ02ℏ2n2E_n=-m_ee^4/8\epsilon_0^2\hbar^2n^2En​=−me​e4/8ϵ02​ℏ2n2, which give a very good description of the hydrogen atomic spectrum. Nevertheless, there are discrepancies. For example, a close examination of the n=2n=2n=2 to n=1n=1n=1 transition shows that it consists of a doublet, which is one of many observations that led Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit to introduce electron spin into quantum mechanics.

It was soon recognized that it was necessary to take into account the fact
that the magnetic moment due to orbital angular momentum and the magnetic moment due to spin angular momentum interact with each other, just as two magnets interact with each other, and the energy of this interaction must be included in the Hamiltonian operator:

H^=−ℏ22me∇2−e24πϵ0r+ξ(r)L^⋅S^\hat H=-\frac {\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2-\frac {e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0r}+\xi(r)\hat {\boldsymbol L}\cdot \hat {\boldsymbol S} H^=−2me​ℏ2​∇2−4πϵ0​re2​+ξ(r)L^⋅S^

The added term is called the spin-orbit interaction form.

Realize that spin-orbit interaction is a small effect in a hydrogen atom, and that the resultant energies are going to differ only slightly from the electrostatic energies En=−mee4/8ϵ02ℏ2n2E_n=-m_ee^4/8\epsilon_0^2\hbar^2n^2En​=−me​e4/8ϵ02​ℏ2n2. We are not able to solve the Schrödinger equation exactly with the spin-orbit interaction term included, but because the effect of the term is small, we expect that the energies En=−mee4/8ϵ02ℏ2n2E_n=-m_ee^4/8\epsilon_0^2\hbar^2n^2En​=−me​e4/8ϵ02​ℏ2n2 will be altered only slightly by its inclusion. We can use a technique called perturbation theory to calculate how much the electrostatic energies are altered (perturbed).

When the spin-orbit interaction is taken into account, L^2\hat L^2L^2 and S^2\hat S^2S^2 no longer commute with H^\hat HH^ and so L\boldsymbol LL and S\boldsymbol SS are no longer conserved; only the total angular momentum is conserved:

J=L+S\boldsymbol J=\boldsymbol L+\boldsymbol S J=L+S

So we have:

J^2∣jmj⟩=ℏ2j(j+1)∣jmj⟩andJ^z∣jmj⟩=ℏmj∣jmj⟩\hat J^2|jm_j\rangle=\hbar^2j(j+1)|jm_j\rangle \quad and \quad \hat J_z|jm_j\rangle =\hbar m_j|jm_j\rangle J^2∣jmj​⟩=ℏ2j(j+1)∣jmj​⟩andJ^z​∣jmj​⟩=ℏmj​∣jmj​⟩

If spin-orbit interaction is considered, then the energy of a hydrogen atom depends upon nnn and jjj. The general theory to determine jjj is a little bit involved, but it’s fairly easy to determine the allowed values of jjj in this case, however, because s=1/2s=1/2s=1/2 and msm_sms​ can assume only two values, ±1/2\pm1/2±1/2.

There are 2l+12l+12l+1 possible orbital angular-momentum states for a fixed value of lll and 2s+1=22s+1=22s+1=2 possible spin angular-momentum states, giving 2(2l+1)2(2l+1)2(2l+1) as the total number of states.

We can summarize all these results in one equation by writing:

j=l+sand∣l−s∣j=l+s \quad and \quad |l-s| j=l+sand∣l−s∣

When l=0l=0l=0, we have j=1/2j=1/2j=1/2; when l=1l=1l=1, we have j=3/2j=3/2j=3/2 and 1/21/21/2; and when l=2l=2l=2, we have j=5/2j=5/2j=5/2 and 3/23/23/2, and so on.

7.7 The Electronic Energy Levels of a Hydrogen Atom Are Described by Term Symbols

We designate the electronic states of a hydrogen atom by an atomic term
symbol, which is expressed by the occupied orbital followed by the symbol 2lj^2l_j2lj​. The left superscript here represents the multiplicity (degeneracy) of the spin, which is 2s+1=22s+1=22s+1=2. In writing a term symbol for atomic hydrogen, we make the correspondence:

Let’s look at the Lyman series, which is the series of lines that arise from transitions from states with n≥2n\ge2n≥2 to the n=1n=1n=1 state. We can use the Rydberg formula to calculate the frequencies of the lines in the Lyman series:

ν~=109 677.58(1−1n2)cm−1n=2,3,⋯\tilde \nu =109\,677.58 \left(1-\frac 1{n^2} \right)cm^{-1} \quad n=2,3,\cdots ν~=109677.58(1−n21​)cm−1n=2,3,⋯

Not all these states can make a transition to the ground state because of selection rules. Recall that selection rules are restrictions that govern the possible, or allowed, transitions from one state to another. In the case of atomic spectra, the selection rules are:

ΔL=±1ΔS=0ΔJ=0,±1\Delta L =\pm1 \\ \Delta S=0 \\ \Delta J=0,\pm1 ΔL=±1ΔS=0ΔJ=0,±1

except that a transition from a state with J=0J=0J=0 to another state with J=0J=0J=0 is not allowed (forbidden). Thus, if we look closely at the Lyman series of atomic hydrogen, we see that the allowed transitions into the ground state are:

Atomic term symbols are sometimes called spectroscopic term symbols because atomic spectral lines can be assigned to transitions between states that are described by atomic term symbols.

The values of the frequencies associated with the 2→12\rightarrow 12→1 transitions can be computed:

ν~=82 258.9206cm−1ν~=82 259.2865cm−1\tilde \nu = 82\,258.9206 cm^{-1} \\ \tilde \nu= 82\,259.2865 cm^{-1} ν~=82258.9206cm−1ν~=82259.2865cm−1

The increased spectral complexity caused by spin-orbit coupling is called fine structure.

The next system to study is the helium atom, whose Schrödinger equation is:
