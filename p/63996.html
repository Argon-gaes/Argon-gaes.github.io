Chapter 6 The Rigid Rotator and Rotational Spectroscopy

The rotation of a diatomic molecule can be described by a rigid rotator, which is like a rotating dumbbell. Using the concept of a reduced mass, we can picture the rotator as a body of reduced mass μ\muμ rotating about a fixed center.

This problem lends itself naturally to spherical coordinates, and the wave functions are functions of θ\thetaθ and ϕ\phiϕ, the two angles in spherical coordinates. The wave functions are called spherical harmonics and occur in many problems involving a fixed center.

Solving the Schrödinger equation for a rigid rotator is a fairly lengthy process, and so in most of this chapter we shall simply present and discuss the energies and the corresponding wave functions.

（ 描述转动问题会很自然地引入球坐标系，后面在解氢原子时也是在球坐标系下进行的。 ）

6.2 The Rigid Rotator Is a Model for a Rotating Diatomic Molecule

We will show in Section 6.7 that the selection rule for the rigid rotator says that transitions are allowed only from adjacent states or that:

ΔJ=±1\Delta J=\pm1 ΔJ=±1

In addition to the requirement that ΔJ=±1\Delta J=\pm1ΔJ=±1, the molecule must also possess a permanent dipole moment to absorb electromagnetic radiation. Thus, HClHClHCl has a pure rotational spectrum, but N2N_2N2​ does not.

In the case of absorption of electromagnetic radiation, the energy difference is:

6.3 Rotational Transitions Accompany Vibrational Transitions

In the previous chapter, we showed that the vibrational motion of a diatomic molecule can be well approximated by a ha1monic oscillator, and in this chapter, we have just seen that the rotational motion of a diatomic molecule can be well represented by a rigid rotator. We combine these ideas and say that the rotational-vibrational motion of a diatomic molecule can be well approximated by the rigid rotator- harmonic oscillator model.

Within the rigid rotator-harmonic oscillator approximation, the rotational and vibrational energy of a diatomic molecule is given by:

Ev,J=(v+12)hν+BJ(J+1)E_{v,J}= \left(v+\frac 12 \right)h\nu+BJ(J+1) Ev,J​=(v+21​)hν+BJ(J+1)

or:

E~v,J=(v+12)ω~+B~J(J+1)v,J=0,1,2,⋯\tilde E_{v,J}= \left(v+\frac 12 \right)\tilde \omega + \tilde BJ(J+1) \quad v,J=0,1,2,\cdots E~v,J​=(v+21​)ω~+B~J(J+1)v,J=0,1,2,⋯

Typical values of ω~\tilde \omegaω~ and B~\tilde BB~ are of the order of 103 cm−110^3\,cm^{-1}103cm−1 and 1 cm−11\,cm^{-1}1cm−1, respectively, so the spacing between vibrational energy levels is about 100 to 1000 times the spacing between rotational levels.

When a molecule absorbs infrared radiation, the vibrational transition is accompanied by a rotational transition. The rotation-vibration spectrum of HBr(g)HBr(g)HBr(g) is shown in Figure 6.4.

The gap centered around 2560 cm−12560\,cm^{-1}2560cm−1 corresponds to the missing line at ω~\tilde \omegaω~, because the transition ΔJ=0\Delta J=0ΔJ=0 is forbidden. On each side of the gap is a series of lines whose spacing is about 10 cm−110\,cm^{-1}10cm−1. The series toward the high-frequency side is called the R branch and is due to rotational transitions with ΔJ=+1\Delta J=+1ΔJ=+1. The series toward the low frequencies is called the P branch and is due to rotational transitions with ΔJ=−1\Delta J=-1ΔJ=−1.

（ 可以从图中看出，振动光谱谱线的能差远大于转动光谱谱线的能差，也就是说在发生振动能级跃迁的同时一定伴随着转动能级跃迁。 ）

But we also see several features we cannot explain. Close examination shows that the lines in the R branch are more closely spaced with increasing frequency and that the lines of the P branch become further apart with decreasing frequency. We will discuss the spacing of the lines in the R and P branches in the next section.

6.5 The Lines in a Pure Rotational Spectrum Are Not Equally Spaced

We can find the line in a pure rotational spectrum aren’t exactly equally spaced as the rigid-rotator approximation predicts. The discrepancy can be resolved by realizing that a chemical bond is not truly rigid. As the molecule rotates more energetically (increasing JJJ), the centrifugal force causes the bond to stretch slightly.

This small effect can be treated by perturbation theory (Chapter 8), and the end result is that the energy can be written as:

6.7 The Selection Rule in the Rigid-Rotator Approximation Is ΔJ=±1\Delta J=\pm1ΔJ=±1

As we said when we discussed the selection rule for a harmonic oscillator in Section 5.12, the probability that a molecule makes a transition from one vibrational state to another when it is irradiated with electromagnetic radiation in the z direction is given by the square of an integral of the form:

⟨v′∣μz∣v⟩=∫−∞∞dxψv′(x)μz(x)ψv(x)\langle v'|\mu_z|v \rangle =\int_{-\infin}^{\infin} dx\psi_{v'}(x)\mu_z(x)\psi_v(x) ⟨v′∣μz​∣v⟩=∫−∞∞​dxψv′​(x)μz​(x)ψv​(x)

In the case of a rigid rotator, the transition dipole moment integral takes the form (we shall derive this result in Section 8.6):

⟨J′,m′∣μz∣J,m⟩=∫02π∫0πYJ′m′(θ,ϕ)∗μzYJm(θ,ϕ)sin⁡θdθdϕ\langle J',m'|\mu_z|J,m \rangle =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}Y_{J'}^{m'}(\theta,\phi)^*\mu_zY_J^m(\theta,\phi) \sin\theta d\theta d\phi ⟨J′,m′∣μz​∣J,m⟩=∫02π​∫0π​YJ′m′​(θ,ϕ)∗μz​YJm​(θ,ϕ)sinθdθdϕ

Using the fact that μz=μ0cos⁡θ\mu_z=\mu_0 \cos\thetaμz​=μ0​cosθ gives:

⟨J′,m′∣μz∣J,m⟩=μ0∫02π∫0πYJ′m′(θ,ϕ)∗YJm(θ,ϕ)cos⁡θsin⁡θdθdϕ\langle J',m'|\mu_z|J,m \rangle =\mu_0\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}Y_{J'}^{m'}(\theta,\phi)^*Y_J^m(\theta,\phi) \cos\theta \sin\theta d\theta d\phi ⟨J′,m′∣μz​∣J,m⟩=μ0​∫02π​∫0π​YJ′m′​(θ,ϕ)∗YJm​(θ,ϕ)cosθsinθdθdϕ

Notice that μ0\mu_0μ0​ must be nonzero for the transition moment to be nonzero. Thus, we have now proven our earlier assertion that a molecule must have a permanent dipole moment for it to have a pure rotational spectrum.

Now we substitute the YJm(θ,ϕ)Y_J^m(\theta,\phi)YJm​(θ,ϕ) into the equation and let x=cos⁡θx=\cos\thetax=cosθ to obtain:

⟨J′,m′∣μz∣J,m⟩=μ0NJ′m′NJm∫02πdϕei(m−m′)ϕ∫−11dx xPJ′∣m′∣(x)PJ∣m∣(x)\langle J',m'|\mu_z|J,m \rangle =\mu_0N_{J'm'}N_{Jm} \int_0^{2\pi}d\phi e^{i(m-m')\phi} \int_{-1}^{1} dx\ x P_{J'}^{|m'|}(x) P_J^{|m|}(x) ⟨J′,m′∣μz​∣J,m⟩=μ0​NJ′m′​NJm​∫02π​dϕei(m−m′)ϕ∫−11​dx xPJ′∣m′∣​(x)PJ∣m∣​(x)

The integral over ϕ\phiϕ is zero unless m=m′m=m'm=m′, so we find that Δm=0\Delta m=0Δm=0 is part of the rigid-rotator selection rule. Integration over ϕ\phiϕ for m=m′m=m'm=m′ gives a factor of 2π2\pi2π, so we have:

⟨J′,m′∣μz∣J,m⟩=2πμ0NJ′m′NJm∫−11dx xPJ′∣m′∣(x)PJ∣m∣(x)\langle J',m'|\mu_z|J,m \rangle =2\pi\mu_0N_{J'm'}N_{Jm} \int_{-1}^{1} dx\ x P_{J'}^{|m'|}(x) P_J^{|m|}(x) ⟨J′,m′∣μz​∣J,m⟩=2πμ0​NJ′m′​NJm​∫−11​dx xPJ′∣m′∣​(x)PJ∣m∣​(x)

We can evaluate this integral in general by using the recursion formula:

(2J+1)xPJ∣m∣(x)=(J−∣m∣+1)PJ+1∣m∣(x)+(J+∣m∣)PJ−1∣m∣(x)(2J+1)xP_J^{|m|}(x)=(J-|m|+1)P_{J+1}^{|m|}(x)+(J+|m|)P_{J-1}^{|m|}(x) (2J+1)xPJ∣m∣​(x)=(J−∣m∣+1)PJ+1∣m∣​(x)+(J+∣m∣)PJ−1∣m∣​(x)

By using this relation, we obtain:

⟨J′,m∣μz∣J,m⟩=2πμ0NJ′mNJm∫−11dx PJ′∣m∣(x)×[(J−∣m∣+1)2J+1PJ+1∣m∣(x)+(J+∣m∣)2J+1PJ−1∣m∣(x)]\begin{aligned} \langle J',m|\mu_z|J,m \rangle= &2\pi\mu_0N_{J'm}N_{Jm}\int_{-1}^1dx\ P_{J'}^{|m|}(x) \\[3mm] &\times \left[\frac {(J-|m|+1)}{2J+1}P_{J+1}^{|m|}(x)+\frac {(J+|m|)}{2J+1}P_{J-1}^{|m|}(x) \right] \end{aligned} ⟨J′,m∣μz​∣J,m⟩=​2πμ0​NJ′m​NJm​∫−11​dx PJ′∣m∣​(x)×[2J+1(J−∣m∣+1)​PJ+1∣m∣​(x)+2J+1(J+∣m∣)​PJ−1∣m∣​(x)]​

Using the orthogonality relation for the PJm(x)P_J^m(x)PJm​(x), we find that the above integral will vanish unless J′=J+1J'=J+1J′=J+1 or J′=J−1J'=J-1J′=J−1. This finding leads to the selection rule J′=J±1J'=J\pm1J′=J±1, or ΔJ=±1\Delta J=\pm1ΔJ=±1.

Thus, we have shown that the selection rule for pure rotational spectra in the rigid-rotator approximation is that the molecule must have a permanent dipole moment and that ΔJ=±1\Delta J=\pm1ΔJ=±1 and Δm=0\Delta m=0Δm=0.

（ 转动光谱的选律也和振动光谱类似，来自于递推关系的限制。 ）

6.8 The Precise Values of the Three Components of Angular Momentum Cannot Be Measured Simultaneously

Angular momentum plays a key role not only in the theory of the rigid rotator, but in the theory of the hydrogen atom as well. To keep things general, we’ll use lll for angular momentum and mmm for its z component in this section.

Recall that angular momentum is a vector quantity. We can write the expression of the operator in spherical coordinates:

L^x=−iℏ(−sin⁡ϕ∂∂θ−cotθ cos⁡ϕ∂∂ϕ)L^y=−iℏ(cos⁡ϕ∂∂θ−cotθ sin⁡ϕ∂∂ϕ)L^z=−iℏ∂∂ϕ\hat L_x=-i\hbar \left(-\sin\phi\frac {\partial}{\partial \theta}-cot\theta\ \cos\phi\frac {\partial}{\partial\phi} \right) \\[3mm] \hat L_y=-i\hbar \left(\cos\phi\frac {\partial}{\partial \theta}-cot\theta\ \sin\phi\frac {\partial}{\partial\phi} \right) \\[3mm] \hat L_z=-i\hbar\frac {\partial}{\partial\phi} L^x​=−iℏ(−sinϕ∂θ∂​−cotθ cosϕ∂ϕ∂​)L^y​=−iℏ(cosϕ∂θ∂​−cotθ sinϕ∂ϕ∂​)L^z​=−iℏ∂ϕ∂​

We see that the spherical harmonics are eigenfunctions of L^z\hat L_zL^z​:

L^zYlm(θ,ϕ)=NlmL^zPl∣m∣(cos⁡θ)eimϕ=NlmPl∣m∣(cos⁡θ)L^zeimϕ=ℏmYlm(θ,ϕ)\begin{aligned} \hat L_zY_l^m(\theta,\phi) &=N_{lm}\hat L_zP_l^{|m|}(\cos\theta)e^{im\phi} \\[3mm] &=N_{lm}P_l^{|m|}(\cos\theta)\hat L_ze^{im\phi} \\[3mm] &= \hbar mY_l^m(\theta,\phi) \end{aligned} L^z​Ylm​(θ,ϕ)​=Nlm​L^z​Pl∣m∣​(cosθ)eimϕ=Nlm​Pl∣m∣​(cosθ)L^z​eimϕ=ℏmYlm​(θ,ϕ)​

This equation shows that measured value of LzL_zLz​ are integral multiples of ℏ\hbarℏ.

However, the spherical harmonics are not eigenfunctions of L^x\hat L_xL^x​ or L^y\hat L_yL^y​.

Because the spherical harmonics are simultaneous eigenfunctions of both L^2\hat L^2L^2 and L^z\hat L_zL^z​, we can determine precise values of L2L^2L2 and LzL_zLz​ simultaneously (Section 4.4), which implies that the operators L^2\hat L^2L^2 and L^z\hat L_zL^z​ commute.

We can prove that ∣m∣≤l|m| \le l∣m∣≤l, or that m=0,±1,±2,⋯ ,±lm=0,\pm1,\pm2,\cdots,\pm lm=0,±1,±2,⋯,±l. It follows:

(L^2−L^z2)Ylm(θ,ϕ)=ℏ2[l(l+1)−m2]Ylm(θ,ϕ)(\hat L^2-\hat L_z^2)Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)=\hbar^2[l(l+1)-m^2]Y_l^m(\theta,\phi) (L^2−L^z2​)Ylm​(θ,ϕ)=ℏ2[l(l+1)−m2]Ylm​(θ,ϕ)

Because:

L^2=L^x2+L^y2+L^z2\hat L^2=\hat L_x^2+\hat L_y^2+\hat L_z^2 L^2=L^x2​+L^y2​+L^z2​

then:

(L^2−L^z2)Ylm(θ,ϕ)=(L^x2+L^y2)Ylm(θ,ϕ)=ℏ2[l(l+1)−m2]Ylm(θ,ϕ)(\hat L^2-\hat L_z^2)Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)=(\hat L_x^2+\hat L_y^2)Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)=\hbar^2[l(l+1)-m^2]Y_l^m(\theta,\phi) (L^2−L^z2​)Ylm​(θ,ϕ)=(L^x2​+L^y2​)Ylm​(θ,ϕ)=ℏ2[l(l+1)−m2]Ylm​(θ,ϕ)

Thus, the observed values of Lx2+Ly2L_x^2+L_y^2Lx2​+Ly2​ are [l(l+1)−m2]ℏ2[l(l+1)-m^2]\hbar^2[l(l+1)−m2]ℏ2. But because Lx2+Ly2L_x^2+L_y^2Lx2​+Ly2​ is the sum of two squared terms, it cannot be negative, and so we have:

[l(l+1)−m2]ℏ2≥0[l(l+1)-m^2]\hbar^2 \ge 0 [l(l+1)−m2]ℏ2≥0

or

l(l+1)≥m2l(l+1) \ge m^2 l(l+1)≥m2

Because lll and mmm are integers, so it says that:

∣m∣≤l|m| \le l ∣m∣≤l

or that the only possible values of the integer mmm are:

m=0,±1,±2,⋯ ,±lm=0,\pm1,\pm2,\cdots,\pm l m=0,±1,±2,⋯,±l

The equation shows that there are 2l+12l+12l+1 values of mmm for each value of lll.

We can prove:

[L^2,L^x]=[L^2,L^y]=[L^2,L^z]=0[\hat L^2,\hat L_x]=[\hat L^2,\hat L_y]=[\hat L^2,\hat L_z]=0 [L^2,L^x​]=[L^2,L^y​]=[L^2,L^z​]=0

But they do not commute among themseles:

[L^x,L^y]=iℏL^z[L^y,L^z]=iℏL^x[L^z,L^x]=iℏL^y\left[\hat L_x,\hat L_y \right] =i\hbar\hat L_z \\[3mm] \left[\hat L_y,\hat L_z \right]=i\hbar\hat L_x \\[3mm] \left[\hat L_z,\hat L_x \right]=i\hbar\hat L_y [L^x​,L^y​]=iℏL^z​[L^y​,L^z​]=iℏL^x​[L^z​,L^x​]=iℏL^y​

It is possible to observe precise values of L2L^2L2 and LzL_zLz​ simultaneously, it is not possible to observe precise values of LxL_xLx​ and LyL_yLy​ simultaneously because they do not commute with each other.

Even though LxL_xLx​ and LyL_yLy​ do not have precise values, they do have an average value:

⟨Lx⟩=⟨Ly⟩=0\langle L_x \rangle=\langle L_y \rangle =0 ⟨Lx​⟩=⟨Ly​⟩=0

A nice classical interpretation of these results is that LLL precesses about the z axis, mapping out the surface of the cone shown there. The average values of ⟨Lx⟩\langle L_x \rangle⟨Lx​⟩ and ⟨Ly⟩\langle L_y \rangle⟨Ly​⟩ are zero. This picture is in nice accord with the uncertainty principle: by specifying LzL_zLz​ exactly, we have a complete uncertainty in the angle ϕ\phiϕ associated with LzL_zLz​.

Before leaving this section, we should address the following question: What is so special about the z direction? The answer is that nothing at all is special about the z direction. We could have chosen either the x or y direction as the unique direction and all the above results would be the same, except for exchanging x or y for z.
